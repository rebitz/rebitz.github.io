I'm broadly interested in how we decide what to look at. At any given moment, our current goals and our evolutionary-hardwired attentional priorities may favor different targets for attention or action. How do we resolve this conflict and combine these influences into unified, actionable signals? How do we adaptively adjust the balance between these priorities in response to the changing demands of the environment? How do these different behavioral states affect our percepts of the world?

Specific projects have examined:

decision-making and attention:
 Little is known about how attention is allocated during complex, naturalistic behaviors such as foraging. My current work seeks to address this question on several complementary levels, from a behavioral examination of how stimulus processing is affected by reward history and decision-states, to neural recordings in the frontal eye fields, a prefrontal oculomotor control region implicated in attention.
arousal and attention:
 In order to adaptively interact with complex, natural environments, animals must allocate limited attentional resources between stimuli that are relevant to their current goals and stimuli that are irrelevant to current goals, but perhaps relevant in some deep evolutionary sense. One simple way to regulate this balance might be through adjusting a global arousal state. However, little is known about how arousal affects attentional priorities. As reported in a recent paper, I found that autonomic arousal (as indexed by pupil size) not only predicts global shifts in distractibility, but also the extent to which salient, social distractors cause greater task interference than nonsocial distractors. A subsequent electrophysiological recording project, now under review, examines how the dorsal anterior cingulate tracks these attentional priorities and predicts task-facilitating adjustments in arousal.
oxytocin in social attention:
 Oxytocin promotes a wide variety of mammalian social behaviors, but the heterogeneity of these effects have frustrated attempts to develop a unified hypothesis of oxytocin's function. In a series of experiments in my graduate work, I found that oxytocin blunts species-typical social attention in the rhesus macacque, obliterating the typical salience of important social actors, such as emotional or high status others. These attentional effects may explain a surprising array of apparently disparate behavioral observations. Because oxytocin is released during positive social interactions, I propose that oxytocin may be a mechanism through which affiliative cues can reduce social vigilance, thereby permitting more relaxed and exploratory social interactions. (I have a few relevant publications on this topic, including a recent review).
