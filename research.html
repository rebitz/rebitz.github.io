Our brains are profoundly flexible. We have the capacity to act in countless unique, creative ways despite identical circumstances. What we decide to do at any given moment is not a deterministic consequence of our environment - instead our goals, expectations, even our level of arousal shape how we process sensory information and make decisions.

My ultimate goal

 is to understand how different states remap how sensation is transformed into action. I'm also interested in how we can adjust our state in order to change our behavior. These are expansive goals, but I believe we can make a lot of progress if we start with a particularly tractable question:

"In Between Days," flickr user Peter Tandlund

how do we control what we look at?

The world is constantly swarming with things that we could be looking at. We know a lot about how we resolve this conflict in order to choose a single eye movement. But - what kinds of network states implement these priorities? How do we adjust these states when our goals or environmental demands change?

My current research

examines how we direct our eyes while we're in different goal states. It turns out that our brains can generate the same eye movement response to the same visual scene in very different ways, depending on our current goal state. Plus, this research has has revealed some basic functional motifs that seem to underlie top-down control and creative, flexible decision-making, respectively.

Methodologically

, I develop behavioral paradigms that encourage transitions between different internal states while monkeys and humans look at things. I probe the mechanisms underlying these states through i) population electrophysiology, ii) electrical microstimulation, and iii) pharmacological challenge. I also like to build models. Whenever possible, I try to identify and exploit endogeous variability in internal states, often through inductively discovering the latent structure in behavior. My work enthusiastically incorporates tools, approaches, and insight from other disciplines, especially artificial intelligence (how do we build agents that can do X?) and ethology (how does X function as a part of species-typical behavior?).
