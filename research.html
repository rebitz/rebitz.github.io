Our brains are profoundly flexible. What we decide to do at any given moment is not a deterministic consequence of our circumstances - instead our goals, expectations, even our level of arousal can shape how we process sensory information and make decisions.

My ultimate goal

 is to understand how different internal states remap how sensation is transformed into action. I'm also interested in how we can adjust our state in order to change our behavior. These are expansive goals, but I have made some progress by starting with one particularly tractable question:

"In Between Days," flickr user Peter Tandlund

how do we control what we look at?

The world is constantly swarming with things that we could be looking at. We know a lot about how we resolve this conflict in order to choose a single eye movement. But - what kinds of network states implement these priorities? How do we adjust these states when our goals or environmental demands change?

My current research

examines how we direct our eyes while we're in different goal states. It turns out that we can generate the same eye movement in response to the same visual scene in very different ways, depending on our current goal state and the changes these goal states cause in the dynamics of the prefrontal cortex.

Methodologically

, I develop behavioral paradigms that encourage transitions between different internal states while monkeys and humans look at things. Whenever possible, I try to identify internal states from first principles: through inductively discovering the latent structure in behavior. I use crowd-sourced psychophysics to rapidly prototype new paradigms. Then, I probe the mechanisms underlying these states through i) population electrophysiology, ii) electrical microstimulation, and iii) pharmacological challenge. I like to build models to make sense of these results and to design new experiments. My work enthusiastically incorporates tools, approaches, and insight from other disciplines, especially artificial intelligence (how would we build agents that can do X?) and ethology (how does X function as a part of species-typical behavior?).
