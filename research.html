Our brains are profoundly flexible. How we see the world (and what we decide to do about it) depends on our goals, expectations, and even our level of arousal. Our lab studies (1) how our internal states change the way we transform sensation into action, and (2) how we adjust our internal states in order to achieve different goals. We're also interested in understanding how control and flexibility evolved with the elaboration of the prefrontal cortex.

Our work shows

that we can generate the same sensorimotor transformation in very different ways, depending on why we're doing it (e.g. 1, 2), that arousal changes our attentional priorities (e.g. 1, 2), that we adjust our internal states in order to set appropriate attentional priorities (e.g. 1, 2, 3), and that the prefrontal control depends on a subsumption motif (e.g. 1).

Methodologically

, our lab combines psychophysics, electrophysiology and perturbations with computational modeling of brain and behavior. We develop behavioral paradigms that encourage transitions between different internal states. On the analysis side, we then work to identify these states from first principles: through characterizing the latent structure in behavior. We enthusiastically incorporate tools, approaches, and insight from other disciplines, especially ethology and artificial intelligence.
